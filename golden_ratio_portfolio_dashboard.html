Sequence of returns risk (SRR) — stress test
The favorable and unfavorable sequences above use identical average annual returns — only the order differs. With no withdrawals, both would produce the same end value. But with a 5% annual withdrawal, early poor returns permanently impair capital before compounding can recover. This is the SRR effect. The golden ratio's 61.8% equity weight creates moderate exposure — less than all-equity, more than a conservative 40/60 portfolio.

Pros & cons
Advantages
Mathematically principled — removes emotional and arbitrary allocation bias
Self-similar structure: same φ ratio governs tier-level and sub-tier splits
61.8% equity delivers strong long-term real return participation
23.6% fixed income provides meaningful drawdown cushion and income
14.6% alternatives adds inflation hedging and low-correlation diversification
Easy to communicate, rebalance, and defend to clients or trustees
φ-based rebalancing bands (±3.8% / ±6.2%) extend the same logic to triggers
Disadvantages
No peer-reviewed evidence φ outperforms MPT-optimized or 60/40 portfolios
Ignores investor risk tolerance, tax situation, liquidity horizon, and liabilities
Static weights do not adapt to interest rate or macroeconomic regimes
61.8% equity = moderate-to-high SRR exposure for anyone actively withdrawing
9% alternatives typically requires access to hedge funds, commodity ETFs, or private vehicles
Tax-loss harvesting, Roth conversions, and RMD rules may force deviation from strict ratios
Forward equity return assumption (~10%) faces headwinds from current valuations
Projections are illustrative estimates using historical return assumptions (US equity 10%, Intl 8%, Treasuries 3.5%, TIPS 4%, Gold 5%, REITs 7%) and do not guarantee future results. SRR scenarios use identical average returns with reversed sequences to isolate the sequence effect. This is educational content, not financial, tax, or legal advice.
